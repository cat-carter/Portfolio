Project Summary

The Problem We Solved

Despite increasing state and local requirements for body-worn camera use, new and less tech-savvy first responders find the camera's user interface hard to navigate — especially during high-pressure situations. This complexity leads to device misuse, recording errors at pivotal moments, difficulty accessing data, and increased apprehension about the technology.

🎥

Selective Evidence Release

Only 42% of body camera footage is currently released to the public, limiting accountability and transparency.

🧠

Cognitive Overload

Officers struggle with complex interfaces during high-stress situations, diverting focus from the incident itself.

🔍

Subjective Interpretation

Footage review lacks contextual clarity and metadata, leading to vastly different assessments by reviewers.

📂

Limited Functionality

Current systems lack features for evidence organization, quick reference, and contextual metadata tagging.

Target Users & Needs

👮 Primary: Law Enforcement Officers

  • Need intuitive, glove-friendly interface for field conditions
  • Require quick access to recording controls during incidents
  • Need clear visual feedback on device status

👁 Secondary: Evidence Reviewers & Public

  • Need searchable, tagged footage with contextual metadata
  • Require transparent access to incident recordings

Design Constraints

Physical hardware limitations Small screen & button controls Extreme conditions (darkness, gloves, stress) Security requirements for sensitive data 12-week academic timeline

Project Goals

① Improve Evidence Accessibility

Historical Context

Body-worn cameras in the U.S. began as a pilot program in 2012 in Phoenix and Mesa, Arizona, and Rialto, California. The Rialto pilot saw significant success — reporting an 88% reduction in complaints against law enforcement officers. However, adoption was slow due to cost concerns.

In 2014, the killing of Michael Brown in Ferguson, Missouri accelerated national interest. President Obama proposed federal reimbursement for half the implementation cost, and the Justice Department awarded $28 million in grants across 32 states. Today, more than half of all states have mandated BWC use by law enforcement.

Despite wider adoption, significant hurdles remain: data storage, training, proper tagging of video evidence, and interfaces that fail to account for stress and divided attention in the field.

Key Research Insights

Rialto Pilot: 88% Reduction in Complaints

The 2012 Rialto, CA pilot program reported an 88% reduction in complaints against officers — one of the strongest early evidence cases for BWC adoption.

Improper Tagging = Lost Evidence

Improper or omitted video tagging is a leading cause of misplacement and accidental deletion of footage after upload, directly undermining accountability.

Design Causes Misuse — Not Just Training

Research shows mounting position and activation methods greatly affect usability. Many errors stem from interface designs that don't account for stress or divided attention (Suss et al., 2018).

AI Activation Reduces Mental Load

Automated and AI-assisted activation systems improve recording consistency and reduce the officer's mental load during high-stress situations (Journal of Criminal Justice, 2025).

Design Precedents

Axon Body 4 (2023)

Industry Standard Law Enforcement Camera
  • Clear indicator light shows recording status at a glance
  • Battery-life display visible without removing device
  • Immediate feedback designed for high-stress situations
  • Large, accessible button placement for field use
Design Takeaway: High-visibility indicators and accessible hardware controls are essential for real-world law enforcement use — informing the recording status and battery indicators in this design.

GoPro Hero7

Consumer-Grade Intuitive Design
  • Touchscreen gestures (swipe, long-press) familiar to smartphone users
  • Large, clear indicator buttons on a small screen
  • Effective use of limited screen space
  • Low learning curve reduces training time
Design Takeaway: Familiar touch interactions and clear button affordances reduce cognitive load — directly inspiring the large button layout and swipe-down settings menu in this design.
Synthesis

The Axon Body 4 and GoPro Hero7 both demonstrate that clear visual indicators, accessible hardware controls, and familiar touch interactions can dramatically reduce cognitive load. This design draws directly from both: high-visibility status indicators from Axon, and intuitive touchscreen layout from GoPro, adapted for law enforcement field conditions.

Key Insights from Task Analysis

Recording Must Be Effortless

Start/stop recording via a physical slider button — a single action with immediate audio and visual feedback (beep + red light).

Settings Need Quick Access

Swiping down from the top opens settings — a familiar gesture that avoids deep menu navigation during active use.

Covert Mode Is Safety-Critical

Disabling indicator lights and sounds for stealth operations is essential and must be quickly toggled — assigned to Catherine's design scope.

Tagging Requires Simplicity

Officers can swipe left on a video and select from prepopulated tags — reducing the manual input that leads to evidence misplacement.

Design Philosophy

"I wanted to keep it minimalist while still providing buttons that gave a lot of information at a quick glance, such as the recording symbol, battery life, time, and notification bell."

This approach directly addresses the cognitive load concerns identified in research, ensuring officers can operate the camera effectively even in high-stress situations.

Major Takeaways & Planned Revisions

1. Terminology Clarity is Critical

"Ghost Mode" consistently confused users → Renamed to "Covert Mode".

2. Media Organization Needs Enhancement

Add search bar, tag filtering, and AI-assisted tagging with speech-to-text.

3. Remove Real-Time Broadcasting

Feature not perceived as valuable → simplify to reduce cognitive load.

4. Strengthen Emergency Feedback

Add stronger visual confirmation, clear messages, and persistent status indicators.

5. Improve Physical Button Integration

Remove redundant on-screen controls and show clear visual feedback for physical button states.

6. Clarify Action Affordances

Replace ambiguous icons (like "+") with labeled action buttons.

Final Prototype

Key Features

A comprehensive body-worn camera system designed to enhance officer safety, evidence integrity, and public accountability.

🎥

Audio & Video Recording

Physical slider button starts/stops recording — a beep sounds and a red light appears confirming recording is active.

🕵️

Incognito / Covert Mode

Disables indicator lights and alert sounds for stealth operations. Accessed via the settings pull-down menu (ninja icon).

🚨

Emergency Response

Physical button hold or home screen tap sends emergency signal, flashes device lights, and automatically begins real-time broadcasting.

📺

Real-Time Broadcasting

Stream live footage from the device via the home screen satellite icon. Triggers automatically during emergency activation.

📸

Media Library & Gallery

View all recorded files organized by date. Swipe left on any video to tag it with prepopulated or custom tags.

🔔

Notifications & Alerts

Bell icon on home screen surfaces all uncleared device notifications and daily alerts.

🔐

Two-Factor Authentication

Required to access recorded files — securing sensitive footage from unauthorized viewing.

⚙️

Settings Menu

Swipe down from the top to access volume, screen brightness, text size, and programmable button configuration.

🔘

Programmable Buttons

Press and hold the side button icons on screen to assign physical device buttons to functions like voice notes or covert mode.

Learnings

Reflections & Next Steps

Through this academic project, our team gained valuable insights into human-centered design principles and the complexities of designing for high-stakes, real-world scenarios.

Design Learnings

1
Terminology Matters

Clear, unambiguous language is critical in high-stress situations. "Ghost Mode" → "Covert Mode" significantly improved comprehension.

2
Test Early and Often

Iterative testing revealed recurring issues across participants, validating the need for systematic refinement.

3
Reduce Cognitive Load

Officers need to focus on situations, not interfaces. Every design decision prioritized simplicity and clarity.

Process Insights

1
Physical-Digital Integration

Understanding hardware constraints and physical button behaviors is essential for cohesive system design.

2
Feedback-Driven Iteration

Professor critique and peer testing identified blind spots we missed, emphasizing the value of diverse perspectives.

3
Designing for Context

Field conditions, gloves, lighting variations, and stress levels all influence interface design choices.
